The Return of the West Seattle Hum: Infrasound, Cargo Ships, and Global Supply Chains

Published: March 19, 2026 | Filed under: Local Mysteries, Urban Acoustics

If you live in West Seattle and have found yourself staring at the ceiling at 3:00 AM wondering if a diesel truck is idling inside your living room, you are not losing your mind. The infamous West Seattle Hum has returned.

This localized acoustic anomaly has been an intermittent plague on the peninsula for over a decade. It is a textbook example of infrasound—extremely low-frequency noise (often 70 Hz or lower)—that easily penetrates walls and windows, turning the very foundation of your home into a resonating speaker box. Because you feel the vibration in your bones just as much as you hear it, standard earplugs and white noise machines are completely useless.

The Prime Suspect: The Industrial Duwamish

While global "Hums" are sometimes blamed on everything from amorous fish to shifting tectonic plates, West Seattle’s acoustic villain has a documented, mechanical origin. Back in 2012, local sleuths and journalists tracked the noise to massive industrial vacuum systems used to offload dry-bulk cargo ships (like those carrying cement and gravel) at facilities such as the CalPortland plant on the Duwamish River.

Following public outcry, custom silencers were installed on the equipment, bringing temporary peace to the area. But if the silencers are still there, why has the hum returned with such vengeance in early 2026?

The 2026 Data Anomaly: A Surge in Breakbulk

To understand the current noise, we have to look at the shipping data. Overall, standard container volume at the Seattle-Tacoma ports has been dropping—it was down 13.9% in January 2026 compared to the previous year. You might expect a quieter port.

However, the specific type of cargo that requires those massive, droning vacuums is known as "breakbulk." According to the Northwest Seaport Alliance (NWSA), while standard containers declined, breakbulk volumes surged by a massive 42.2% in January 2026. You aren't just hearing general port noise; you are hearing the acoustic fallout of a highly localized explosion in dry-cargo offloading.

It is a fascinating theory, but the timeline requires a slight adjustment. The massive U.S. military campaign against Iran, Operation Epic Fury, officially commenced on February 28, 2026. Because the 42.2% spike in West Seattle's breakbulk cargo occurred in January, the initial surge was actually driven by "vessel call scheduling and cargo timing factors," according to the NWSA.

However, the theory holds weight for the future. With the Red Sea and Strait of Hormuz now severely compromised by the February/March conflict, global shipping networks are facing massive detours. Moving forward, this will almost certainly cause "vessel bunching"—where delayed bulk carriers arrive in Seattle in condensed, erratic clusters, forcing the offloading vacuums to work overtime.

Down the Rabbit Hole: Weird Facts and Theories About The Hum

If you are lying awake listening to this mechanical drone, congratulations: you have unwittingly joined a very bizarre, highly exclusive club. The "Hum" is a documented global phenomenon, and the science surrounding it gets weird quickly.

The 2% Club and the Ambidextrous Anomaly

Only about 2% to 4% of the global population has the physiological auditory sensitivity to hear the Hum. Even stranger, Dr. Glen MacPherson, who runs the World Hum Map and Database Project, discovered a baffling statistical anomaly: there are eight times as many ambidextrous people among Hum-hearers as there are in the general population. Science currently has no explanation for this correlation.

The Earplug Paradox

Because the Hum operates at such a low frequency (often 30 to 60 Hz), it easily penetrates solid objects, including the human skull. For many sufferers, aggressively soundproofing a room or wearing heavy-duty earplugs actually makes the Hum seem louder. By blocking out normal, higher-frequency environmental noises, the brain is left alone in a quiet room with the low-frequency vibration physically resonating through the bones of the skull via bone conduction.

The Submarine VLF Theory

A prevailing fringe hypothesis suggests some localized Hums aren't acoustic sound waves at all, but rather electromagnetic energy. Global militaries use massive transmitters to blast Very Low Frequency (VLF) radio waves around the world to communicate with submerged submarines. Some researchers suspect that certain human bodies can absorb these VLF waves and interpret them as an acoustic drone—a variation of the microwave auditory effect (also known as the Frey effect).

The Gas Pipeline Crusader

The 2019 documentary Doom Vibrations follows mechanical engineer Steve Kohlhase, who spent a decade investigating the Hum. His theory? The massive compressors used to push gas through interstate pipelines create a low-frequency wave that essentially turns the earth itself into a giant subwoofer. (Interestingly, current local forums show a Pacific Northwest filmmaker named Duncan Dickerson is actively soliciting stories from sleep-deprived residents to make a new documentary specifically about the West Seattle hum!)

It Actually Was Fish That One Time

While the West Seattle hum is almost certainly industrial, the infamous "horny fish" theory wasn't pulled out of thin air. In the 1980s, residents of houseboats in Sausalito, California, were tormented by a nighttime drone. Marine biologists eventually discovered the culprit: the male Plainfin Midshipman fish. To attract mates, the males vibrate their swim bladders for up to an hour at a time. The frequency perfectly matched the resonant frequency of the boat hulls, effectively turning the houseboats into giant acoustic amplifiers for an underwater mating ritual.

What You Can Do About It

Until the global shipping lanes normalize and the breakbulk backlog clears, the hum will likely persist. If you want to confirm the source of your sleeplessness and help the community, try these steps:

  • Track the Ships: Bookmark a live marine tracker like VesselFinder. When the hum wakes you up, check the map. If there are bulk carriers moored on the Duwamish, you have your culprit.
  • Report It: You can log your experience globally at TheHum.info to contribute to ongoing acoustic research.
  • Change Your Sleep Geography: Low-frequency waves resonate most powerfully through the ground. If possible, avoid sleeping in basements or on ground floors, and move your bed away from exterior walls.
  • Log Your Local Experience: Keep a record of the dates and times the hum is most intense. Community boards are actively using this data to petition for stricter nighttime industrial noise ordinances.
